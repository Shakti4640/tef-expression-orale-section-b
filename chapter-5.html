Chapter 5: Phase 2 — Presenting the Opportunity (45–60 Seconds)

📦 SECTION SUMMARY

What this chapter covers: The second phase of your Section B conversation — how to deliver the essential details of the activity (what, where, when, cost, special features) in a way that BUILDS ENTHUSIASM rather than sounding like a factsheet. The information delivery structure, the art of highlighting selling points, the critical technique of connecting the activity personally to your friend, and how to plant seeds for arguments you will need later during objection handling.

Why this matters: The Presentation phase is where most candidates either shine or stumble. The strong candidate weaves details into an exciting narrative that makes the friend WANT to go. The weak candidate reads a list of facts that sounds like a travel brochure. The difference is not the information itself — both candidates have the same advertisement — but HOW the information is delivered. This phase also plants the seeds for your later arguments. A detail mentioned casually here ("and they lend you all the equipment") becomes a powerful counter when the friend later says "But I don't have the right gear."

Connection to previous chapters: Chapter 4 created curiosity — your opening hooked the friend and made them want to know more. This chapter DELIVERS on that curiosity. But it delivers strategically, not all at once. You are still building momentum toward the objection-handling phase (Chapter 6 and beyond), where the real persuasion battle happens. Think of the Presentation as loading your arsenal — every detail you mention here becomes ammunition you can use later.

Difficulty Level: ⭐⭐⭐ (Requires balancing information delivery with enthusiasm, strategic detail selection, and smooth integration with your opening — more complex than it appears)

1️⃣ THE PURPOSE OF THE PRESENTATION PHASE

The Presentation phase sits between your opening hook and the objection-handling battle. Its purpose is NOT simply to inform. If information were the goal, you could hand the friend the advertisement and let them read it. The purpose is to FRAME the information in a way that maximizes desire and minimizes resistance.

Every detail you present should accomplish one of three things:

║                                                                  ║

║     → "The concept is incredible — you learn from a real pro    ║
║       and then you get to keep everything you make!"            ║

║       "I'm bad at this" before the friend even says it          ║

║     → Make the friend feel this activity was MADE for them      ║
║     → "With your artistic eye, you're going to be amazing       ║
║       at this!"                                                  ║

The Address Analogy

Think of the advertisement as a house. It has many rooms — date, time, location, price, features, requirements. The advertisement gives you a floor plan. But a floor plan does not sell a house. What sells a house is a guided tour where someone walks you through each room and says "Imagine waking up to THIS view" and "This is where you could put your piano" and "The kitchen gets the most beautiful morning light."

You are giving a guided tour of the activity. Not a floor plan.

2️⃣ THE INFORMATION DELIVERY STRUCTURE

There is a natural order in which information should be delivered. This order mirrors how people naturally process new ideas — from the general concept to the specific details, from the exciting to the practical.

The WWWWP Sequence

╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║                                                                  ║
║  W — WHAT is it?          (The concept — what you will DO)      ║
║      ↓                                                           ║
║  W — WHERE is it?         (The location — but made appealing)   ║
║      ↓                                                           ║
║  W — WHEN is it?          (The timing — framed as convenient)   ║
║      ↓                                                           ║
║  W — WHY is it special?   (The unique selling point)            ║
║      ↓                                                           ║
║  P — PERSONAL connection  (Why THIS friend should do THIS)      ║
║                                                                  ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

This is NOT a rigid script. You do not need to deliver these five elements in this exact order every time. But you DO need to cover all five, and this order works naturally because each element builds on the previous one.

Why This Order Works

WHAT comes first because the friend needs to understand the basic concept before any other detail matters. If they do not know what the activity IS, the location and date are meaningless.

WHERE comes second because once they know what it is, their next natural question is "Where?" Location can be either a selling point ("right in our neighborhood!") or a potential objection ("it is an hour away") — either way, address it early.

WHEN comes third because timing determines feasibility. "This Saturday" creates urgency. "Next month" gives them time to plan. Frame the timing as convenient, not as a constraint.

WHY IT IS SPECIAL comes fourth because this is your elevator pitch — the one thing that makes this activity different from everything else. This is the detail that makes the friend think "Hmm, that IS interesting."

PERSONAL CONNECTION comes last because it is the emotional climax of your presentation. After you have laid out the facts, you end with the most powerful element — why this specific activity matters for this specific friendship.

The "Seed Planting" Technique

Sometimes you want to MENTION a detail casually in the presentation — not as a counter-argument, but as a seed that you can reference later. This makes your later counter-argument feel like a natural callback rather than a prepared rebuttal.

Seed in Presentation (Phase 2):

"Et le truc cool, c'est que c'est vraiment ouvert à tout le monde — y a pas de niveau requis."

This is mentioned casually, as part of the general description. Not emphasized. Not argued. Just stated.

Harvest during Objection Handling (Phase 3):

Examiner: "Je suis vraiment nul en travail manuel..."

You: "Mais tu te souviens ce que je t'ai dit ? C'est ouvert à TOUT LE MONDE. Y a aucun niveau requis. C'est justement fait pour des gens comme nous qui ont jamais touché à un tour de potier !"

Now your counter-argument feels organic — you are referencing something you ALREADY said, not pulling out a new fact from nowhere. This creates coherence (Chapter 2's fourth criterion) because the conversation feels like a unified whole, not disconnected segments.

💡 THE SEED RULE: In your presentation, casually plant 1-2 seeds that you anticipate needing later. Do not over-plant — if you address every possible objection preemptively, the examiner has nothing to object to, and you lose the objection-handling phase where most points are earned. Plant just enough to give yourself callbacks.

5️⃣ THE PRICE PRESENTATION — A SPECIAL CASE

Price deserves its own section because it is the detail most likely to trigger an objection, and HOW you present the price dramatically affects whether that objection arises and how strong it is.

The Wrong Way to Present Price

"Ça coûte 45 euros."

This is a raw number thrown at the friend with no context. Their brain immediately evaluates: "45 euros — is that a lot or a little?" And for most people, the default reaction to any price stated in isolation is "that seems expensive."

The Right Way to Present Price

Never state the price alone. ALWAYS surround it with VALUE context.

Technique 1: The Breakdown

Break the total price into smaller, more digestible components:

"Pour 45 euros, t'as trois heures de cours avec un pro, tous les ingrédients, ET le repas à la fin. Quand tu divises, ça fait genre 15 euros de l'heure — c'est rien du tout !"

What this does: 45€ sounds like a lot. 15€ per hour sounds reasonable. The math has not changed, but the PERCEPTION has.

Technique 2: The Comparison

Compare the price to something the friend already spends money on without thinking:

"C'est même pas le prix d'un dîner au resto — sauf que là on apprend quelque chose en plus !"

What this does: Anchors the price to a familiar expenditure, making it feel normal rather than exceptional.

Technique 3: The Value Stack

List everything included in the price to make it feel like a bargain:

"Quarante euros et t'as TOUT : le matériel, les matériaux, la cuisson, et tu repars avec ta création. Si tu devais acheter tout ça séparément, ça te coûterait trois fois plus."

What this does: Makes the friend see the price as a package deal rather than a single expense.

Technique 4: The Emotional Reframe

Shift the conversation from cost to value in emotional terms:

"C'est un investissement sur nous-mêmes et sur notre amitié. On peut pas mettre un prix sur les souvenirs qu'on va créer."

What this does: Moves the evaluation from "is this worth 45€?" to "is this experience worth having?" — a question with only one answer.

Technique 5: The Delay

If the price is genuinely high and you know it will trigger resistance, consider NOT mentioning it in the presentation at all. Let the friend ask. This gives you the advantage of responding to a specific question rather than preemptively defending a number.

In presentation: "Le prix est super raisonnable, surtout vu tout ce qui est inclus."

When they ask: "C'est combien exactement ?"

Your response: "45 euros — mais attends, avant que tu réagisses, pense à ce que ça inclut..."

💡 THE PRICE PRINCIPLE: Never let a number stand alone. Numbers without context trigger resistance. Numbers WITH context trigger evaluation — and when the value is clear, the evaluation is favorable.

7️⃣ THE PRESENTATION PACING — NOT TOO FAST, NOT TOO SLOW

Your presentation should last 45-60 seconds. This is a guideline, not a rigid rule, but the range matters:

Under 30 seconds: You did not give enough information. The friend does not have a clear picture of the activity. You rushed through facts without building enthusiasm. The presentation feels incomplete.

45-60 seconds: Ideal. Enough time to cover the WWWWP structure, highlight the key selling point, and create personal connection. Not so long that you are monologuing.

Over 90 seconds: You are delivering a speech, not having a conversation. The examiner is sitting there waiting to interact. You are losing Interaction Quality points with every second you deny them a chance to respond.

The Breathing Points

Within your 45-60 second presentation, create natural BREATHING POINTS — moments where the examiner could jump in if they want to. These are not full stops. They are micro-pauses, tag questions, or tone shifts that signal "I'm open to you reacting."

Breathing Point TypeExampleWhat It Does
Tag question"...tu vois le genre ?" / "...pas mal, non ?"Invites a quick reaction without stopping the flow
Micro-pause after a key detail"C'est quarante euros tout compris. (beat) Et en plus..."Gives them a moment to process
Check-in question"Tu me suis ?" / "Ça te parle ?"Shows you care about their engagement
Reaction invitation"Attends, le meilleur c'est que..."Signals something exciting is coming — they lean in

💡 THE BREATHING POINT BALANCE: Include 2-3 breathing points in your presentation. Fewer than that and you are monologuing. More than that and your presentation feels fragmented — you never build momentum because you keep interrupting yourself.

What Happens If the Examiner Interrupts Your Presentation

Sometimes the examiner will jump in DURING your presentation with an early objection or a question. This is GOOD. It means they are engaged. Do NOT try to finish your presentation first. RESPOND to what they said.

You are presenting: "...et le meilleur c'est que—"

Examiner interrupts: "Attends, c'est combien ?"

WRONG response: "Attends, laisse-moi finir. Donc le meilleur c'est que..."

RIGHT response: "Quarante euros tout compris — matériel, matériaux, cuisson, tout ! Et en plus, tu repars avec ta création. C'est une affaire, non ?"

Then you can continue with whatever you had planned to say. The conversation has simply moved from Phase 2 to Phase 3 earlier than expected. That is fine. Flexibility earns Interaction Quality points. Rigidity loses them.

8️⃣ THE ENTHUSIASM CALIBRATION

Throughout your presentation, enthusiasm is your fuel. But enthusiasm needs to be CALIBRATED — consistently high but with variation. Constant maximum enthusiasm becomes noise. Varied enthusiasm creates a dynamic, engaging delivery.

The Enthusiasm Wave

Think of your presentation's energy like a wave, not a flat line:

Energy
  ↑
  │    ★ KEY SELLING        ★ PERSONAL
  │    ★ POINT               ★ CONNECTION
  │   /  \                  /  \
  │  /    \    ★ DETAIL    /    \
  │ /      \  /   \       /      \
  │/        \/     \     /        \
  │  INTRO         LOGISTICS       CLOSE
  └──────────────────────────────────→ Time

Lower energy for logistics: When you mention the date, time, and location, you can be slightly more matter-of-fact. These are practical details that do not need hype.

Higher energy for selling points: When you reveal the special feature or the unique concept, your energy should RISE. This signals to the friend "THIS is the exciting part."

Highest energy for personal connection: When you tell them why this is perfect for THEM, your energy should peak. This is the emotional climax of your presentation.

Beyond intonation, specific WORDS carry enthusiasm:

Enthusiasm LevelWords and ExpressionsExample

Very Highde dingue, de folie, de ouf, hallucinant, exceptionnel"L'ambiance a l'air de dingue !"

⚠️ THE SUPERLATIVE TRAP: Using superlatives for everything ("the BEST," "the MOST AMAZING," "the MOST INCREDIBLE") devalues them. If everything is "the best," nothing is. Save your strongest enthusiasm language for one or two moments. The rest should be "high" but not "peak." This contrast makes the peak moments genuinely impactful.

9️⃣ THE PERSONAL CONNECTION — THE MOST POWERFUL ELEMENT

Of all five elements in the WWWWP structure, the Personal Connection is the most persuasive. Facts about the activity can be interesting. But making the friend feel that this activity was CHOSEN FOR THEM — that you thought of them specifically, that their unique qualities make them perfect for this — is what creates emotional investment.

Three Levels of Personal Connection

Level 1: Generic Connection (Weak)

"On va bien s'amuser ensemble."

This could be said about ANY activity to ANY friend. There is nothing personal about it. It is a default phrase that shows no thought about the specific person or the specific activity.

Level 2: Activity-Specific Connection (Better)

"Tu adores la cuisine italienne, donc ça va te plaire."

This connects the friend's known interest to the activity. Better, because it shows you thought about THEM. But it is still relatively surface-level.

Level 3: Deep Personal Connection (Powerful)

"Franchement, avec ton œil artistique et ta patience — tu sais, la façon dont tu peux passer des heures à dessiner dans ton carnet — je suis sûr que tu vas avoir un talent naturel pour la poterie. C'est le même genre de créativité, juste avec les mains au lieu d'un crayon."

This connects a SPECIFIC personality trait of the friend to a SPECIFIC aspect of the activity, with a SPECIFIC observation that shows deep knowledge of who they are. This feels genuine. This feels like a real friend who really knows you and really thinks you would love this.

💡 THE PERSONAL CONNECTION PRINCIPLE: The more SPECIFIC your personal connection, the more powerful it is. "We'll have fun" is generic. "With your amazing sense of rhythm, you're going to be the star of the dance class" is specific, flattering, and personal. It makes the friend feel SEEN — and people who feel seen are much harder to say no to.

🔟 THE TRANSITION TO OBJECTION HANDLING

Your presentation ends. The friend now has a clear picture of what you are proposing. This is the moment when the examiner transitions into their role as the reluctant friend. They will raise their first objection.

How to Read the Transition

The examiner's first response after your presentation will signal the start of Phase 3. It typically takes one of these forms:

Examiner ResponseWhat It MeansYour Next Move
"Hmm, ça a l'air pas mal, mais..."Mild interest + incoming objectionLet them finish, then deploy RARE (Chapter 3)
"Ouais, je sais pas trop..."Hesitation — not a clear objection yetAsk "Qu'est-ce qui te fait hésiter ?" to draw out the real concern
"C'est combien ?"Practical question — not necessarily an objectionGive the price with VALUE CONTEXT (Technique 1-4 from Section 5 of this chapter)
"Oui mais [specific objection]"Direct objectionDeploy RARE immediately
"C'est quand exactement ?"Information-seekingAnswer, then redirect: "C'est samedi prochain — ça tombe bien, non ?"

The Worst Thing You Can Do at This Transition

The worst thing you can do when the examiner raises their first objection is PANIC. Some candidates interpret the first "but..." as failure — as if the friend SHOULD have said yes after the presentation. No. The objection is EXPECTED. It is DESIGNED. It is the beginning of the most important phase of the conversation. Welcome it.

When you hear the first objection, your internal response should be: "Good. Now I get to show what I can do."

1️⃣3️⃣ KEY TAKEAWAYS FROM CHAPTER 5

1. The Presentation phase delivers essential details while BUILDING ENTHUSIASM — not reading a factsheet. Every detail should build excitement, pre-empt objections, or create personal connection.

2. Follow the WWWWP structure: What → Where → When → Why Special → Personal Connection. Not rigidly, but as a natural guide.

3. Use PRESENTATION PHRASES to deliver information conversationally: "Le truc génial, c'est que..." / "Ce qui m'a accroché, c'est..." / "Ça m'a tout de suite fait penser à toi..."

4. Strategically DISTRIBUTE details across three categories: Present Now (Phase 2), Counter-Argument Ammunition (Phase 3), and Closing Weapons (Phase 5). Do not use everything at once.

5. PLANT SEEDS — casually mention 1-2 details that you can reference later during objection handling. This creates coherence and makes your counter-arguments feel organic.

6. NEVER state a price alone. Always surround it with value context: breakdown, comparison, value stack, emotional reframe, or delay.

7. Calibrate enthusiasm like a wave — moderate for logistics, high for selling points, peak for personal connection. Do not stay at maximum the entire time.

8. The Personal Connection is your most powerful tool. Make it SPECIFIC to this friend and this activity. "With your [specific trait], you're going to [specific impressive result]" is infinitely more powerful than "We'll have fun."

9. Keep the presentation to 45-60 seconds. Include 2-3 breathing points where the examiner could react. If they interrupt with a question or objection, RESPOND — do not force-complete your planned presentation.

10. When the examiner raises their first objection after your presentation, do not panic. This is the EXPECTED transition to Phase 3 — the phase where most of your points are earned. Welcome it.
