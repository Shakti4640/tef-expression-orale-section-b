3ï¸âƒ£ THE SIX STRATEGIC ANGLES FOR MONEY OBJECTIONS

Just as time objections had five angles (Chapter 9), money objections have six. The first four address "WON'T PAY," the last two address "CAN'T PAY."

Angle 1: VALUE PROPOSITION â€” Show What They Get

The Logic: "When you look at everything included, the price is actually a bargain."

When It Works Best: When the activity includes multiple elements (materials, food, instruction, equipment) that the friend may not have considered. You break down the total package to show the price is justified.

Why It Works Psychologically: People evaluate price in isolation. "50 euros" sounds like a lot. But "50 euros for 3 hours of professional instruction, all ingredients included, AND you eat what you cook" suddenly sounds reasonable. You are not changing the PRICE â€” you are changing the FRAME.

Angle 2: COMPARISON â€” Put the Cost in Context

The Logic: "You spend this much on things that give you less â€” at least this creates a memory."

When It Works Best: When the friend regularly spends money on restaurants, delivery, streaming services, or other discretionary spending. You show that the activity costs the SAME as things they already buy without thinking twice.

Why It Works Psychologically: Comparison destroys the illusion of "expensive." 50 euros feels expensive in a vacuum. 50 euros compared to "what we spent at that restaurant last week" feels normal. The friend cannot argue that 50 euros is too much for an experience when they spent 50 euros on takeout last Tuesday.

Angle 3: INVESTMENT REFRAME â€” It's Not a Cost, It's an Investment

The Logic: "This money isn't being wasted â€” it's buying you skills, memories, or personal growth."

When It Works Best: When the activity teaches something (workshop, class, course) or creates lasting memories (concert, trip, unique experience). You shift the mental category from "expense" to "investment."

Why It Works Psychologically: Expenses feel like losses. Investments feel like gains. When you buy a coffee, the money is gone and the coffee is consumed. When you "invest" in a cooking class, you gain a SKILL you keep forever. The money is gone either way, but the framing changes how it FEELS.

Angle 4: DISCOUNT OR DEAL MENTION â€” The Price Is Actually Lower

The Logic: "There's a promotion / group rate / early bird discount that makes it cheaper than you think."

When It Works Best: When the advertisement actually mentions a discount, or when you can credibly invent one for the roleplay. This is a FACTUAL counter â€” the price the friend is reacting to is not the real price.

Why It Works Psychologically: People love deals. The exact same item at 50 euros feels expensive, but at "40 euros with the 20% early bird discount" feels like a steal. The friend shifts from "should I spend this?" to "I'd be stupid NOT to take advantage of this deal."

Angle 5: OFFER TO PAY â€” Remove the Barrier Entirely

The Logic: "I'll cover your share. Your company matters more to me than the money."

When It Works Best: ONLY when the friend has clearly signaled they CANNOT afford it (Category B). This is your nuclear option for money objections â€” it eliminates the obstacle completely. But it must be delivered with warmth, not pity.

Why It Works Psychologically: When a friend offers to pay, two things happen. First, the financial barrier disappears. Second, the friend feels valued â€” someone cares enough about their company to spend money on them. The key is HOW you say it. "I'll pay because you're broke" is humiliating. "I'll pay because I really want us to do this together" is generous.

Angle 6: COST REDUCTION â€” Find Ways to Spend Less

The Logic: "We can reduce the overall cost by sharing transport, bringing our own food, splitting costs differently."

When It Works Best: When the friend is budget-conscious and would respond well to practical solutions. This shows you are sensitive to their financial reality while still making the activity possible.

Why It Works Psychologically: The friend feels HEARD. You are not dismissing their budget concern â€” you are WORKING WITH IT. You are showing that their financial comfort matters to you, and that you are willing to adapt the plan to accommodate them.
