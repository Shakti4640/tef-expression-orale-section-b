Chapter 10: Time Objections Part 2 — "Débordé," "Pas le bon moment," & "J'ai des plans"

📦 SECTION SUMMARY

What this chapter covers: Three specific TIME sub-categories that require fundamentally different approaches from the general "I don't have time" covered in Chapter 9. When the examiner says "I'm swamped with work," they need a DIFFERENT response than when they say "I already have plans." Same broad category (time), completely different psychology, completely different counters.

Why these need their own chapter: In Chapter 9, we handled the GENERAL time objection — "I don't have time" as a vague statement. But examiners rarely stay vague. They get SPECIFIC. They tell you WHY they do not have time. And each specific reason demands a tailored response. A candidate who gives the same answer to "I'm overwhelmed at work" and "I already have plans for Saturday" sounds like a robot reading from a script. A candidate who adapts to the SPECIFIC concern sounds like a real person in a real conversation.

The three sub-categories:

  • "Je suis débordé(e) / J'ai trop de travail" — The work overload objection (7 sentences)
  • "C'est pas le bon moment" — The bad timing objection (5 sentences)
  • "J'ai déjà des plans" — The schedule conflict objection (5 sentences)

Total new sentences: 17 (Sentences #16–32)

Connection to Chapter 9: All 15 idioms from Chapter 9 remain available and will be deployed in new contexts here. The 15 counter-sentences from Chapter 9 also remain in your toolkit — you may combine Chapter 9 sentences with Chapter 10 sentences in a single conversation. Think of Chapter 9 as your GENERAL arsenal and Chapter 10 as your SPECIALIZED arsenal.

Difficulty Level: ⭐⭐⭐ (Higher than Chapter 9 because these objections are more specific, which means generic responses will not work — you must LISTEN carefully and respond precisely)

2️⃣ SUB-CATEGORY A: "JE SUIS DÉBORDÉ(E) / J'AI TROP DE TRAVAIL"

Understanding This Objection

This is NOT the same as "I don't have time." This is "I am OVERWHELMED." The difference is emotional. "I don't have time" is a scheduling problem. "I'm swamped" is a STRESS problem. The friend is not just busy — they are drowning. They feel pressure, anxiety, maybe even guilt about not being productive enough.

This matters because your counter-strategy must address the EMOTION, not just the logistics. Telling someone who is drowning "the pool is only 2 meters deep" does not help. You need to throw them a lifeline.

The Strategic Approach

When someone says "I'm overwhelmed with work," your best angles are:

AngleLogicWhy It Works on THIS Objection
Reframe the break as productive"Taking a break makes you MORE effective"Speaks to their guilt about not working — you are giving them PERMISSION to stop
Acknowledge their stress, then redirect"I know you're under pressure — that's exactly why you need this"Validates their reality instead of dismissing it, then flips it
Separate the permanent from the temporary"Work will always be there — this won't"Shows that work is infinite but this opportunity is finite
Use humor to lighten the weight"Your boss can survive without you for two hours"Breaks the tension of their stress — laughter releases pressure

The 7 Counter-Sentences (#16–22)

Counter #16 — The Productivity Argument

"Raison de plus pour prendre une vraie pause ! Ton cerveau a besoin de souffler pour être productif."

Translation: All the more reason to take a real break! Your brain needs to breathe to be productive.

When to use: This is your go-to FIRST response when the friend mentions work overload. It is the perfect opening counter because it does something unexpected — it AGREES that work is important, then argues that the break SERVES the work.

What makes it effective: The phrase "Raison de plus" (All the more reason) is one of the most powerful pivoting expressions in French. It takes the friend's objection and instantly converts it into an argument FOR your position. Instead of fighting against "I have too much work," you ride WITH it — "Yes, you have too much work, and THAT IS WHY you need this."

The psychology: People who are overworked often feel GUILTY about taking breaks. They feel they "should" be working. By framing the break as something that makes them MORE productive, you remove the guilt. You are not asking them to abandon their work — you are helping them work BETTER.

The grammar to notice: "Raison de plus pour + infinitive" — a sophisticated construction. "Ton cerveau a besoin de souffler" — the verb "souffler" (to breathe/take a breather) is colloquial and natural. "Pour être productif" — pour + infinitive expressing purpose.

💡 "RAISON DE PLUS" — YOUR SECRET WEAPON: This expression deserves special attention because it works with EVERY objection category, not just time. "It's expensive" → "Raison de plus pour profiter de la promo !" "I'm tired" → "Raison de plus pour se changer les idées !" "I'm not good at it" → "Raison de plus pour apprendre !" Memorize this expression. It is the Swiss Army knife of Section B.

Counter #17 — The Empathy-First Approach

"Je comprends que t'es sous pression, mais c'est justement pour ça que cette sortie te ferait du bien."

Translation: I understand you're under pressure, but that's precisely why this outing would do you good.

When to use: When the friend sounds genuinely stressed — not just busy, but emotionally strained. This counter leads with EMPATHY, which is essential when someone is overwhelmed.

What makes it effective: The structure is a textbook RARE method execution. "Je comprends que t'es sous pression" is the RECOGNIZE step — you are validating their feelings. "Mais c'est justement pour ça que" is the ADDRESS + REDIRECT — the word "justement" (precisely/exactly) creates the pivot. "Te ferait du bien" is warm and caring — you are not pushing an activity, you are prescribing wellness.

The grammar to notice: "Te ferait du bien" — conditionnel présent of "faire." Using the conditional here is important — it softens the statement from "this WILL do you good" (certainty) to "this WOULD do you good" (suggestion). The conditional is more respectful of the friend's autonomy. This subtle tense choice demonstrates B2+ awareness.

Counter #18 — The Monday Argument

"Le travail sera toujours là lundi. Mais cette occasion, elle, elle attend pas."

Translation: Work will still be there Monday. But this opportunity, it won't wait.

When to use: When the friend specifically mentions a work task that could be done later. This creates a direct contrast between the PERMANENT (work) and the FLEETING (opportunity).

What makes it effective: The sentence structure creates a rhetorical seesaw. One side: work — always there, heavy, permanent, boring. Other side: opportunity — fleeting, exciting, temporary, now-or-never. The friend is forced to choose, and when framed this way, the choice becomes obvious.

The grammar to notice: "Sera toujours là" — futur simple. "Elle, elle attend pas" — the doubled pronoun "elle, elle" is a spoken French emphasis technique. In writing, you might say "cette occasion n'attend pas." In speech, repeating the pronoun creates oral emphasis. This is the kind of natural spoken French that impresses examiners.

What makes this sentence special: Notice how SHORT it is. After longer, more elaborate counters (#16 and #17), dropping a short, punchy statement like this creates rhythmic variety. Not every response needs to be 4 sentences long. Sometimes the most powerful thing is a single, sharp truth.

Counter #19 — The Science Card

"Tu sais ce qui est prouvé scientifiquement ? Les gens qui prennent des pauses sont plus efficaces après."

Translation: You know what's scientifically proven? People who take breaks are more effective afterwards.

When to use: When the friend is the rational, logical type — someone who responds better to FACTS than to emotions. Invoking "science" gives your argument an air of objective authority.

What makes it effective: The opening "Tu sais ce qui est prouvé scientifiquement ?" is a rhetorical question that creates curiosity. The friend's brain automatically wants to hear what comes next. Then you deliver the punch line — breaks make you MORE productive, not less. This is actually true (research on cognitive breaks supports it), which gives you genuine confidence when saying it.

The grammar to notice: "Ce qui est prouvé" — relative clause with "ce qui" (what is...). "Les gens qui prennent des pauses" — another relative clause with "qui." "Sont plus efficaces" — comparative structure. This sentence packs three different grammar structures into a natural-sounding statement.

A caution: Do not overuse the "science says" approach. One mention per conversation is enough. If you keep citing "studies" and "science," it starts to sound like you are giving a lecture, not having a conversation with a friend.

Counter #20 — The Pattern Recognition

"T'es toujours débordé(e), mais t'arrives toujours à t'en sortir. Accorde-toi ce moment !"

Translation: You're always swamped, but you always manage to get through it. Give yourself this moment!

When to use: When you want to both VALIDATE the friend's hard work AND push them to take a break. This counter works by acknowledging their pattern (always busy) and their competence (always survives) to argue that this time is no different.

What makes it effective: The double "toujours" creates a pattern recognition. "You're ALWAYS busy, but you ALWAYS manage." The friend hears their own resilience reflected back to them. Then "Accorde-toi ce moment" is a warm imperative — not "you should take a break" but "GIVE YOURSELF this moment." The reflexive "s'accorder" implies self-care, self-worth, self-permission.

The grammar to notice: "T'arrives toujours à t'en sortir" — the expression "s'en sortir" (to manage/to get through it) is essential informal French. "Arriver à + infinitive" means "to manage to." "Accorde-toi" — imperative of a reflexive verb with the pronoun "toi" after the verb.

Counter #21 — The Health Reality Check

"Une pause de quelques heures ne va pas faire s'écrouler ta carrière, mais ça peut sauver ta santé mentale !"

Translation: A break of a few hours won't make your career collapse, but it might save your mental health!

When to use: When the friend seems to equate ANY break with professional failure. This counter uses gentle exaggeration ("s'écrouler ta carrière") to show the absurdity of their fear, then pivots to something real (mental health).

What makes it effective: The contrast between the dramatic ("career collapsing") and the genuine ("mental health") creates a moment of clarity. The friend realizes they are catastrophizing — treating a 2-hour break as if it were career suicide. The humor of the exaggeration breaks their spiral of stress-thinking.

The grammar to notice: "Ne va pas faire s'écrouler" — futur proche + causative "faire" + reflexive infinitive. This is a genuinely complex grammatical construction. "Faire s'écrouler" means "to cause to collapse." Using this structure naturally is a clear C1 marker.

Counter #22 — The Boss Humor

"Écoute, si ton patron peut survivre sans toi pendant que tu dors, il peut survivre pendant qu'on s'amuse un peu."

Translation: Listen, if your boss can survive without you while you sleep, he can survive while we have a little fun.

When to use: When the tone allows for humor. This is a lighter counter that works well after more serious arguments. It breaks the tension of work-stress talk.

What makes it effective: The logical parallel is airtight and funny. If your boss manages 8 hours every night without you (while you sleep), then 2 more hours will not destroy anything. The humor comes from the absurdity of the implied alternative — that the friend should never sleep either, just in case work needs them.

The grammar to notice: "Si ton patron peut survivre... il peut survivre..." — si + present → present (factual conditional). "Pendant que tu dors... pendant qu'on s'amuse" — parallel "pendant que" clauses create rhythmic balance.

How to Chain Sub-Category A Responses

In a real conversation, the examiner might stay on the "work overload" theme for 2-3 exchanges. Here is how to escalate through these counters:

EXCHANGE 1: Examiner says "J'ai trop de boulot"
→ Deploy Counter #16 (Raison de plus — productivity argument)

EXCHANGE 2: Examiner pushes back "Non mais vraiment, j'ai un deadline..."
→ Deploy Counter #18 (Work will be there Monday) + Counter #17 (empathy first)

EXCHANGE 3: Examiner still resisting "Je peux pas me permettre de perdre du temps"
→ Deploy Counter #21 (career won't collapse) or Counter #22 (boss humor)
→ Combine with Idiom #8 from Chapter 9 (Après la pluie, le beau temps)

3️⃣ SUB-CATEGORY B: "C'EST PAS LE BON MOMENT"

Understanding This Objection

"It's not the right time" is the VAGUEST of the three sub-categories. The friend is not telling you WHY the timing is bad. They are not citing work, not citing other plans — just a general feeling that "now is not the time."

This vagueness is actually your ADVANTAGE. Because they have not given you a specific obstacle, you have two options. You can either PROBE for the real reason (ask what makes it a bad time) or ATTACK the vagueness itself (argue that there is never a perfect time).

The Strategic Approach

AngleLogicWhy It Works on THIS Objection
Challenge the concept of "perfect timing""The perfect moment doesn't exist — waiting means waiting forever"Directly addresses the vagueness by showing it is a trap
Reference past postponements"You said this last time too, and we ended up regretting it"Creates social accountability — the friend does not want to repeat the pattern
Redefine the moment"The right time is when the opportunity is here — and it's HERE"Flips the frame — instead of waiting for the right time, you declare THIS is the right time
Probe for the real reason"What makes it not the right time exactly?"Forces the friend to either get specific (giving you a concrete objection to counter) or admit they do not have a real reason

The 5 Counter-Sentences (#23–27)

Counter #23 — The Perfect Moment Myth

"Y a jamais de moment parfait ! Si on attend les étoiles alignées, on attend toute notre vie."

Translation: There's never a perfect moment! If we wait for the stars to align, we'll wait our whole lives.

When to use: As your FIRST response to any "bad timing" objection. This is a powerful universal truth that is almost impossible to argue against.

What makes it effective: The image of "waiting for the stars to align" is vivid, slightly humorous, and universally understood. It paints the friend's waiting strategy as absurd without being mean about it. "Toute notre vie" adds dramatic weight — you are not just waiting days, you are waiting FOREVER.

What makes this the strongest "bad timing" counter: It does not address a SPECIFIC timing problem (because the friend did not give one). Instead, it attacks the entire CONCEPT of waiting for perfect timing. This forces the friend to either agree (in which case you have won) or get specific about what makes THIS time bad (in which case you now have a concrete objection you can counter with other tools).

The grammar to notice: "Si on attend... on attend..." — si + present → present (general truth conditional). "Les étoiles alignées" — past participle used as adjective. The informal "Y a" instead of "Il n'y a" sets the right register.

Counter #24 — The Déjà Vu Call-Out

"C'est exactement ce que tu disais la dernière fois, et on a fini par regretter de pas y être allés."

Translation: That's exactly what you said last time, and we ended up regretting not going.

When to use: When you want to create a narrative of PAST REGRET to prevent FUTURE REGRET. This works whether or not the friend actually said this before — in the exam context, you are creating a shared history with the examiner.

What makes it effective: Referencing a "last time" creates social pressure through PRECEDENT. The friend does not want to repeat a mistake they have already made. The word "exactement" (exactly) emphasizes that this is a PATTERN, not a one-time thing.

The grammar to notice: "On a fini par regretter" — the structure "finir par + infinitive" (to end up doing) is an important B2 expression. "De pas y être allés" — negative infinitive + y + passé composé infinitive. This is grammatically complex and demonstrates advanced control. The placement of "pas" before the infinitive group (rather than splitting "ne...pas" around it) is natural spoken French.

💡 CREATING SHARED HISTORY IN THE EXAM: You and the examiner do not have a real shared past. But in the ROLEPLAY, you are old friends. You can freely reference "last time," "remember when," "you always say that." The examiner will play along because it enriches the conversation. Sentences like "Tu te souviens la dernière fois qu'on a dit ça ?" create depth and authenticity.

Counter #25 — The Seize the Day Declaration

"Le bon moment, c'est celui qu'on décide de prendre. Et moi je décide que c'est maintenant !"

Translation: The right moment is the one we decide to take. And I'm deciding it's NOW!

When to use: When you want to shift from REACTIVE (answering their objection) to PROACTIVE (declaring a new reality). This counter is assertive, confident, and energetic.

What makes it effective: It redefines "the right moment" entirely. The friend was waiting for external conditions to be perfect. You are saying that the right moment is an INTERNAL decision, not an external circumstance. "Moi je décide" adds personal force — you are not just suggesting, you are DECLARING.

The grammar to notice: "C'est celui qu'on décide de prendre" — demonstrative pronoun "celui" + relative clause. "Moi je" — the stressed pronoun "moi" before "je" adds emphasis. This is a spoken French technique for assertiveness.

Counter #26 — The Opportunity Reframe

"Tu sais quoi, le bon moment c'est quand l'opportunité se présente. Et elle est là, devant nous !"

Translation: You know what, the right moment is when the opportunity presents itself. And it's right here, in front of us!

When to use: When the activity is genuinely time-limited or rare. This reframes the question from "Is this a good time for ME?" to "Is this a good time for this OPPORTUNITY?" — and the answer is obviously yes, because the opportunity is HERE.

What makes it effective: "Elle est là, devant nous" creates a sense of physical presence — as if the opportunity is a living thing standing in front of them, and they are about to let it walk away. The personification of the opportunity makes the stakes feel real and immediate.

The grammar to notice: "C'est quand l'opportunité se présente" — a defining relative structure. "Se présenter" — reflexive verb meaning "to present itself." "Devant nous" — prepositional phrase creating vivid imagery.

Counter #27 — The Balance of Reasons

"On peut toujours trouver une raison de reporter. Mais là, les raisons d'y aller sont plus fortes."

Translation: We can always find a reason to postpone. But here, the reasons to go are stronger.

When to use: When the friend has given multiple small reasons for not going, none of which are individually strong. This counter acknowledges that yes, there are reasons to say no — but argues that the reasons to say YES outweigh them.

What makes it effective: The word "toujours" (always) gently exposes the friend's habit of finding excuses. "Les raisons d'y aller sont plus fortes" uses a COMPARATIVE structure that frames the decision as a weighing exercise. You are not denying the friend's concerns — you are saying that your arguments are heavier on the scale.

The grammar to notice: "On peut toujours trouver" — present tense for general truth. "Les raisons d'y aller" — infinitive after preposition "de" with the pronoun "y" replacing "à cet événement." "Sont plus fortes" — comparative adjective. The sophistication here is in the LOGIC structure, not just the grammar.

4️⃣ SUB-CATEGORY C: "J'AI DÉJÀ DES PLANS"

Understanding This Objection

This is the most CONCRETE of the three sub-categories. The friend is not saying "I'm busy" vaguely or "it's not the right time" abstractly. They are saying "I have SOMETHING ELSE already scheduled." This means you are not fighting against vagueness or stress — you are fighting against a SPECIFIC COMPETING COMMITMENT.

This changes your strategy entirely. You cannot argue "the perfect moment doesn't exist" because the friend HAS a specific moment — it is just already taken. You cannot argue "take a break from work" because their plans might not be work-related. You need new tools.

The Strategic Approach

AngleLogicWhy It Works on THIS Objection
Probe the plans"What are your plans? Maybe we can work around them"Gets specific information you can work with
Compare importance"Are your plans reschedulable? Because this isn't"Argues that YOUR activity is more time-sensitive
Suggest combining"Can we do both? Your thing first, then ours?"Removes the either/or and creates both/and
Suggest including"Bring whoever you're seeing — the more the merrier!"Dissolves the conflict by merging the two plans
Suggest partial attendance"Even if you come late, it's better than nothing"Offers a compromise that respects their existing commitment

The 5 Counter-Sentences (#28–32)

Counter #28 — The Investigator

"C'est quoi tes plans ? Peut-être qu'on peut combiner les deux ou décaler un peu ?"

Translation: What are your plans? Maybe we can combine the two or shift things around a bit?

When to use: As your FIRST response to any "I have plans" objection. This is not a counter-argument — it is a QUESTION. And that is exactly why it works.

What makes it effective: Instead of immediately trying to convince the friend to cancel their plans (which feels aggressive), you ask for INFORMATION. This shows respect for their existing commitment. The offer to "combine or shift" signals flexibility and creativity — you are a problem-solver, not a bulldozer.

Why this question is strategically brilliant: The friend's answer gives you CRUCIAL information for your next move. If they say "I'm seeing Sophie for coffee," you can suggest bringing Sophie along or meeting after coffee. If they say "I have a dentist appointment," you can suggest going to the activity after. If they say "I just want to relax," then you know this is actually a FATIGUE objection (Chapter 15), not a scheduling conflict — and you can switch strategies.

The grammar to notice: "Combiner les deux" — infinitive after "on peut." "Décaler un peu" — the verb "décaler" (to shift/reschedule) is a useful vocabulary word for any scheduling discussion.

Counter #29 — The Reschedulability Argument

"Tes plans, c'est reportable ? Parce que ça, ça l'est pas — c'est une date unique !"

Translation: Are your plans reschedulable? Because this isn't — it's a one-time date!

When to use: When the activity is genuinely a one-time event (concert, special workshop, limited promotion). This creates a direct comparison between two commitments — one that can be moved and one that cannot.

What makes it effective: The word "reportable" (reschedulable) is the key. It frames the friend's plans as FLEXIBLE and your activity as FIXED. If the friend's plans can be moved to next week, but your concert is THIS Saturday only, the logical conclusion is clear — move the flexible thing, attend the fixed thing.

The grammar to notice: "C'est reportable ?" — adjective derived from "reporter" (to postpone). "Ça, ça l'est pas" — the pronoun "l'" replaces "reportable" (ça ne l'est pas → informal: ça l'est pas). This pronominal replacement demonstrates advanced grammar naturally. "Une date unique" — adjective after noun.

Counter #30 — The Gentle Cancel Request

"Ah mince ! Et si tu annulais juste cette fois ? Je te promets que ça vaut le coup."

Translation: Oh darn! What if you canceled just this once? I promise it's worth it.

When to use: When the friend's existing plans seem relatively casual (not a medical appointment or family obligation). The "Ah mince !" at the beginning shows you are genuinely disappointed, which creates emotional pressure.

What makes it effective: "Et si tu annulais" uses the IMPERFECT after "et si" — a softened suggestion structure. It is much gentler than "Annule tes plans !" (Cancel your plans!). The word "juste cette fois" (just this once) minimizes the request — you are not asking them to become unreliable, you are asking for ONE exception. "Je te promets que ça vaut le coup" stakes your personal credibility.

The grammar to notice: "Et si tu annulais" — et si + imparfait is the standard structure for gentle suggestions in French. "Et si on allait au cinéma ?" (What if we went to the movies?). This is a B2 structure that shows you know how to use the imperfect for more than just past narration.

💡 "ET SI + IMPARFAIT" — THE GENTLE SUGGESTION FORMULA: This structure is incredibly useful across all Section B scenarios, not just schedule conflicts. "Et si on essayait ?" (What if we tried?). "Et si on y allait juste une heure ?" (What if we went for just an hour?). It proposes without demanding. Memorize this structure.

Counter #31 — The Late Arrival Compromise

"Écoute, on peut peut-être y aller après ton truc ? Même si on arrive un peu en retard, c'est mieux que rien !"

Translation: Listen, maybe we can go after your thing? Even if we arrive a little late, it's better than nothing!

When to use: When the friend's existing plans happen BEFORE the activity, making a late arrival possible. This shows maximum flexibility — you are willing to adapt your plan to fit around theirs.

What makes it effective: "Après ton truc" is casual and warm — "truc" (thing/stuff) is very informal, which maintains the friendly register. "Même si on arrive un peu en retard" preemptively addresses the friend's worry about showing up late. "C'est mieux que rien" is a pragmatic argument that is hard to dispute — some participation IS better than none.

The grammar to notice: "Même si on arrive" — "même si" (even if) + present indicative (not subjunctive — this is a common error to avoid). "C'est mieux que rien" — comparative with "que."

Counter #32 — The Expansion Invite

"Tes plans, c'est avec qui ? Peut-être qu'ils veulent venir aussi ! Plus on est de fous, plus on rit !"

Translation: Your plans, who are they with? Maybe they want to come too! The more the merrier!

When to use: When the friend's plans are social (meeting another friend, hanging out with someone). This dissolves the conflict entirely by MERGING the two plans. Instead of choosing between you and Sophie, the friend brings Sophie along.

What makes it effective: This is creative problem-solving at its best. The friend presented an either/or situation (your plans vs. my plans). You transformed it into a both/and situation (everyone comes together). The expression "Plus on est de fous, plus on rit" (The more the merrier — literally "the more crazy people there are, the more we laugh") is a well-known French proverb that adds warmth and humor.

The grammar to notice: "C'est avec qui ?" — informal question structure (formal: "Avec qui est-ce ?"). "Plus on est de fous, plus on rit" — the "plus... plus..." comparative structure. "Ils veulent venir aussi" — present tense with "aussi" (also/too) at the end.

💡 "PLUS ON EST DE FOUS, PLUS ON RIT" — This proverb is a GOLD-STANDARD idiom for Section B. It is cheerful, inclusive, and universally known by French speakers. Using it naturally signals cultural fluency. It works any time you suggest adding more people to the plan.

5️⃣ COMPLETE DIALOGUES — ALL THREE SUB-CATEGORIES IN ACTION

Dialogue A: The Work Overload Scenario

Context: You want your friend to come to a weekend yoga class.

You (Opening):
"Thomas ! J'ai trouvé un cours de yoga super bien, c'est tous les samedis matin de 9h à 10h30 juste à côté de chez nous. C'est exactement ce qu'il nous faut pour décompresser !"

Examiner (Work overload objection):
"J'aimerais bien, mais franchement, je suis débordé en ce moment. J'ai un projet énorme au boulot, je sais même pas comment je vais m'en sortir..."

Detection: Work overload objection + emotional stress. Lead with empathy.

You (Counter #17 + Counter #16):
"Je comprends que t'es sous pression, c'est vraiment pas facile ce que tu vis en ce moment. [Empathy — Counter #17] Mais justement, raison de plus pour prendre une vraie pause ! [Counter #16] Tu sais, ton cerveau il tourne en boucle quand tu forces trop. Une heure et demie de yoga, ça te remet les idées en place et tu reviens au bureau lundi avec l'esprit clair. C'est pas du temps perdu, c'est du temps investi. Tu vois ce que je veux dire ?"

Examiner (Pushes back):
"Oui mais j'ai des deadlines, je peux pas me permettre de perdre du temps..."

Detection: Still work-focused, mentioning deadlines specifically. Address the deadline.

You (Counter #18 + Counter #19 + Idiom #8 from Chapter 9):
"Le travail sera toujours là lundi, mais ta santé mentale, elle attend pas. [Counter #18] Et puis, tu sais ce qui est prouvé scientifiquement ? Les gens qui font des pauses régulières respectent MIEUX leurs deadlines que ceux qui travaillent non-stop. [Counter #19] T'as eu une semaine de dingue — après la pluie, le beau temps ! [Idiom #8] Ce cours de yoga, c'est ton rayon de soleil. Allez, une heure et demie, ça change tout."

Examiner (Softening):
"C'est vrai que je suis tendu en ce moment..."

You (Close with Counter #22 + warmth):
"Exactement ! Et ton patron peut survivre sans toi un samedi matin, je te le garantis. [Counter #22] On y va ensemble, on décompresse, et je t'offre un brunch après. T'as plus aucune excuse !"

Dialogue B: The Bad Timing Scenario

Context: You want your friend to come to a photography exhibition.

You (Opening):
"Écoute, il y a une expo photo incroyable ce mois-ci au musée — des photographes de rue du monde entier. Avec ta passion pour la photo, c'est fait pour toi !"

Examiner (Bad timing objection):
"Hmm, c'est pas vraiment le bon moment pour moi en ce moment..."

Detection: Vague "bad timing" — no specific reason given. Challenge the concept.

You (Counter #23 + Counter #25):
"Y a jamais de moment parfait ! Si on attend les étoiles alignées, on attend toute notre vie. [Counter #23] Et puis, le bon moment, c'est celui qu'on décide de prendre. [Counter #25] L'expo ferme à la fin du mois, après c'est fini. Qu'est-ce qui fait que c'est pas le bon moment exactement ? [Probe for the real reason]"

Examiner (Gets slightly more specific):
"Je sais pas, j'ai plein de trucs en tête, j'ai pas la tête à ça..."

Detection: This is actually revealing an EMOTIONAL state, not a scheduling problem. The friend is mentally preoccupied. Adapt.

You (Counter #24 + Idiom #9 from Chapter 9):
"C'est exactement ce que tu disais le mois dernier quand j'avais proposé le ciné, et on a fini par regretter. [Counter #24] Je sais que t'as plein de choses en tête, mais justement, une sortie culturelle c'est le meilleur moyen de prendre du recul. Le temps file — cette expo sera plus là dans deux semaines. [Idiom #9] On y va, on se balade tranquillement, on regarde les photos, on discute. Zéro pression. Ça te dit ?"

Dialogue C: The Existing Plans Scenario

Context: You want your friend to come to a cooking class this Sunday.

You (Opening):
"Marie ! J'ai trouvé un atelier de pâtisserie ce dimanche — on apprend à faire des macarons avec un chef pâtissier ! Tu imagines ?"

Examiner (Existing plans objection):
"Oh, ça a l'air super, mais j'ai déjà prévu de voir Sophie dimanche..."

Detection: Specific schedule conflict — seeing Sophie. Probe and suggest combining.

You (Counter #28 + Counter #32):
"Ah, Sophie ! C'est quoi votre programme exactement ? Peut-être qu'on peut combiner les deux ? [Counter #28] Ou mieux — invite Sophie avec nous ! Plus on est de fous, plus on rit ! [Counter #32] Je suis sûre que Sophie adorerait apprendre à faire des macarons. Et après l'atelier, on va prendre un café toutes les trois. C'est pas une super journée ça ?"

Examiner (Considers but hesitates):
"Hmm, je sais pas si Sophie serait intéressée..."

Detection: The friend is shifting from "I can't" to "I'm not sure" — she is almost convinced. Push gently.

You (Counter #29 + Counter #31):
"Demande-lui ! Le pire qu'elle peut dire c'est non, et dans ce cas tu la vois après l'atelier. [Problem-solving] L'atelier c'est de 10h à 13h — tu peux voir Sophie l'après-midi. [Counter #31 — late combination] Mais l'atelier, c'est ce dimanche uniquement, c'est une date unique ! [Counter #29] Ton café avec Sophie, vous pouvez le reporter à n'importe quand. Allez, envoie-lui un message maintenant, on verra ce qu'elle dit !"

7️⃣ COMMON MISTAKES SPECIFIC TO THESE SUB-CATEGORIES

Mistake #1: Treating All Three Sub-Categories the Same

❌ WRONGWhy It Fails
Examiner says "J'ai déjà des plans avec Sophie" and you respond "Ton agenda sera jamais parfaitement vide !"The friend DOES have a specific plan. The issue is not an empty schedule — it is a CONFLICTING schedule. Your response does not address their actual concern.
Examiner says "J'ai trop de boulot" and you respond "C'est quoi tes plans ?"The friend just told you — WORK. Asking "what are your plans?" sounds like you were not listening.
✅ RIGHTWhy It Works
Examiner says "J'ai des plans avec Sophie" → You respond "Invite Sophie avec nous !"Directly addresses the specific conflict with a creative solution.
Examiner says "J'ai trop de boulot" → You respond "Raison de plus pour prendre une pause !"Directly addresses work stress by reframing the break as beneficial.

🔴 THE RULE: Listen to the SPECIFIC words the examiner uses and respond to THOSE WORDS. If they say "work," talk about work. If they say "Sophie," talk about Sophie. If they say "not the right time," challenge the concept of right timing. MATCH YOUR RESPONSE TO THEIR CONCERN.

Mistake #2: Being Insensitive to Real Stress

When the examiner says "Je suis débordé(e)," they might sound genuinely stressed (remember, they are acting). If you immediately launch into "Come on, it's only two hours!" without acknowledging their stress, you sound uncaring. The friend is telling you they are struggling. VALIDATE that before you counter it.

The fix is simple: always lead with empathy for work overload objections.

❌ Cold✅ Warm
"Mais c'est que deux heures !""Je sais que c'est dur en ce moment. Mais justement, deux heures de décompression, ça peut tout changer."
"Tu travailles trop, viens t'amuser.""Je comprends la pression que tu ressens. C'est exactement pour ça que t'as besoin de cette pause."

Mistake #3: Asking Someone to Cancel Important Plans

If the examiner says "J'ai rendez-vous chez le médecin samedi," do NOT say "Annule ton rendez-vous !" That would be inappropriate even in a roleplay. Instead, suggest attending after the appointment, or ask when the appointment ends. Show that you RESPECT important commitments while still finding a way to make your plan work.

Type of PlanYour Approach
Casual social (coffee with a friend)Suggest combining, rescheduling, or including
Semi-important (gym, errands)Suggest rescheduling to another day
Important (medical, family obligation)Suggest attending the activity BEFORE or AFTER
Very important (funeral, emergency)Acknowledge gracefully and suggest another date — this is the ONE case where you adapt your plan, not theirs

8️⃣ THE ESCALATION MAP — NAVIGATING MULTIPLE TIME OBJECTIONS

Sometimes the examiner will throw MULTIPLE time objections in sequence. They start with "I'm busy," you counter, then they say "And I already have plans," you counter, then they say "It's just not the right time." Here is how to navigate this:

╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║                                                                  ║
║  OBJECTION 1: "Je suis débordé(e)"                             ║

║                                                                  ║

║                                                                  ║

║                                                                  ║

Notice how each response uses a DIFFERENT angle:

  • Response 1: The break helps your work (productivity angle)
  • Response 2: We can work around your schedule (flexibility angle)
  • Response 3: The perfect moment is a myth (philosophical angle)

Three different objections, three different angles, three different counters. THAT is what scores maximum points for Interaction Quality and Coherence.

🔟 KEY TAKEAWAYS FROM CHAPTER 10

1. Specific time objections require specific responses. "I'm swamped at work," "It's not the right time," and "I already have plans" are three DIFFERENT problems requiring three DIFFERENT solutions.

2. For WORK OVERLOAD: Lead with empathy, then reframe the break as productive. "Raison de plus" is your most powerful pivoting expression.

3. For BAD TIMING: Challenge the concept of perfect timing. "Y a jamais de moment parfait" is almost impossible to argue against.

4. For EXISTING PLANS: Probe first (what plans?), then suggest combining, rescheduling, late arrival, or expanding the group. Never demand someone cancel important commitments.

5. "Et si + imparfait" (What if you...) is the gentlest way to suggest changes to someone's plans. Memorize this structure.

6. "Plus on est de fous, plus on rit" (The more the merrier) is a gold-standard idiom for dissolving schedule conflicts.

7. ALWAYS match your response to the examiner's specific words. If they mention "Sophie," your response should mention Sophie. If they mention "deadline," your response should address the deadline.

8. When the examiner throws MULTIPLE time objections in sequence, use a DIFFERENT angle each time. Never repeat the same approach.

9. Chapters 9 and 10 together give you a complete TIME objection toolkit: 32 counter-sentences + 15 idioms + 5 power combinations. This covers every possible time-related resistance you will encounter.

10. With time objections fully mastered, you are ready for the second most common category: MONEY. Chapter 11 handles "C'est trop cher" — where the friend resists because of cost.

Time is abstract and flexible. Money is concrete and finite. The strategies that work for time do NOT work for money. You need new tools.
